Pronunciation & Phonetics

European Portuguese
European Portuguese is different from Brazilian Portuguese in one key way: vowels are swallowed. Unstressed vowels are reduced or dropped entirely. This is why EP sounds fast and "closed" compared to Brazilian. Once you hear this pattern, everything starts to click.
Consonant Rules
Es- → SH-
Words starting with es- or ex- begin with a "sh" sound.
estar → SHtar · esperar → SHperar · escola → SHcola · excelente → eeSHlente
c + a/o/u → K
The letter c before a, o or u makes a hard K sound.
casa → Kaza · como → Komoo · curto → Koortoo
c + e/i → S
The letter c before e or i makes an S sound.
cedo → Sedoo · cidade → Seedade
ç → S
Cedilha always makes an S sound, regardless of the vowel that follows.
coração → koo-ra-Soun · açúcar → a-Sookar
nh → NY
Like the Spanish ñ — a nasal "ny" sound.
sonho → son-NYoo · banho → ban-NYoo · minha → mee-NYa
lh → LY
Like the Italian gli — a "ly" sound.
bolha → bô-LYa · rolha → rô-LYa · trabalho → tra-ba-LYoo
ch → SH
Always a "sh" sound in EP (never "ch" as in English).
chá → SHaa · chamar → SHamar · cheio → SHayoo
x → SH (often)
In many words, x makes a "sh" sound.
caixa → kai-SHa · baixo → bai-SHoo
g + e/i → ZH
Soft G before e or i — like the French j in "je".
gente → ZHente · girassol → ZHirassol
gu + e/i → G (hard)
The u is silent — keeps the G hard before e or i.
guerra → Gerra · guia → Gia · guitarra → Gitarra
J → ZH
Always the French-style "zh" sound (like the s in "measure").
janela → ZHanela · João → ZHowão
qu + e/i → K
The u is silent before e or i.
que → Keh · quem → Kaim · quinto → Kintoo
Vowel & Nasal Rules
-ão → nasal OWN
A nasal "own" sound — like saying "own" through your nose.
são → sown · não → nown · coração → koo-ra-sown
-em / -ens → -AIN
Words ending in -em or -ens are pronounced with a nasal "-ain" sound.
também → tam-bain · ninguém → nin-gain · homens → omainsh
Swallowed vowels
Unstressed e and o at the end of words are often barely pronounced or completely dropped.
como → comm · onde → ond' · grande → grand'
Estou / Está
In natural speech these contract. Estar forms lose the "E" entirely.
Estou → 'tow or SHtow · Está → 'tá or SHtá
Numbers (1, 2, 6)
Key number pronunciations that catch learners out.
um → oom · uma → oo-ma · dois → doysh · seis → saysh
-able → -ável
Many English -able words become -ável in Portuguese (singular) / -áveis (plural).
comfortable → confortável · available → disponível

Grammar Rules

Ser vs Estar — the Big One
SER — permanent identity
Use ser for things that define what something IS: nationality, profession, personality, origin, material, permanent characteristics, time/dates.
Eu sou português. · Ela é médica. · O carro é vermelho.I am Portuguese. · She is a doctor. · The car is red (its colour).
ESTAR — temporary state or location
Use estar for how something IS RIGHT NOW: mood, health, location, temporary conditions, ongoing actions.
Estou cansado. · Ela está em Lisboa. · O café está frio.I am tired (right now). · She is in Lisbon. · The coffee is cold (got cold).
FICAR — to stay, to end up, to become
Ficar often sits between ser and estar. It describes a change of state or staying somewhere.
Fiquei contente. · Vou ficar em casa. · Ficou zangado.I became/ended up happy. · I'll stay home. · He got angry.
Articles
Definite articles agree with the noun
o / a (the, singular) · os / as (the, plural). In Portuguese, you use the article before names and at the start of sentences more than in English.
O Barrie é canadiano. · Adoro a praia. · Os amigos chegaram.Barrie is Canadian. · I love the beach. · The friends arrived.
Indefinite articles
um / uma (a/an) · uns / umas (some). Gender must match the noun.
Um café, por favor. · Uma mesa para dois.A coffee, please. · A table for two.
Contractions — must learn these
Preposition + article contractions
In Portuguese, prepositions fuse with the article that follows them. You cannot separate them.
em + o = no · em + a = na · em + os = nos · em + as = nas de + o = do · de + a = da · de + os = dos · de + as = das a + o = ao · a + a = à · a + os = aos · a + as = às por + o = pelo · por + a = pela · por + os = pelos · por + as = pelas Fui ao mercado. → Fui a + o mercado. I went to the market.
Por vs Para
PARA — destination, purpose, recipient, deadline
Think: heading somewhere, or meant for someone/something.
Vou para Lisboa. · Este livro é para ti. · Para quando? · Estudo para aprender.I'm going to Lisbon. · This book is for you. · By when? · I study (in order) to learn.
POR — reason, exchange, duration, movement through
Think: because of, in exchange for, during/over a period, passing through somewhere.
Obrigado por tudo. · Por quanto? · Passamos pelo parque. · Fiz isso por ti.Thank you for everything. · For how much? · We walked through the park. · I did it because of you.
Adjectives
Adjectives come AFTER the noun (usually)
The default position is noun first, adjective second. This is the opposite of English. The adjective must also agree in gender and number with the noun.
um carro vermelho · uma casa bonita · amigos simpáticosa red car · a beautiful house · friendly friends
Adjective gender agreement
Most adjectives: masculine ends in -o, feminine ends in -a. Adjectives ending in -e don't change. Plural: add -s (or -es if ending in consonant).
cansado / cansada / cansados / cansadas · feliz / felizestired (m/f/m.pl/f.pl) · happy (sg/pl)
Possessives
Possessives agree with what is OWNED, not the owner
This is very different from English. The possessive changes to match the noun being possessed, not the person who owns it.
o meu carro · a minha casa · os meus filhos · as minhas amigasmy car · my house · my sons · my (female) friends

Key Tenses & Structures
Present Continuous — Estar + a + infinitive
For what is happening RIGHT NOW.
Estou a comer. · Ela está a falar. · Estamos a estudar.I am eating. · She is speaking. · We are studying.
Near Future — Ir + infinitive
Like "going to" in English. Very common in everyday speech.
Vou comer. · Vamos ao mercado. · Ela vai telefonar.I'm going to eat. · We're going to the market. · She's going to call.
Simple Past (Pretérito Perfeito)
For completed actions. Key irregular forms: fui (I went/was), fiz (I did), tive (I had), disse (I said), vim (I came).
Fui ao ginásio. · Comemos juntos. · Ela disse que sim.I went to the gym. · We ate together. · She said yes.
Imperfect Past — ongoing or habitual past
For what used to happen or was ongoing in the past. Formed by adding -ava/-ava/-ávamos/-avam (AR) or -ia/-ias/-íamos/-iam (ER/IR).
Quando era criança, vivia em Portugal. · Ele trabalhava todos os dias.When I was a child, I lived in Portugal. · He used to work every day.
Conditional — gostaria / faria / poderia / deveria
For polite requests and hypotheticals. These are the conditional forms of gostar, fazer, poder, dever.
Gostaria de um café. · Poderia repetir? · Deveria ir ao médico.I would like a coffee. · Could you repeat? · I should go to the doctor.
Reflexive Verbs
Actions done to oneself. The reflexive pronoun (me, te, se, nos) normally comes AFTER the verb in EP (but before in questions and negatives).
Chamo-me Barrie. · Levantei-me cedo. · Não me lembro.My name is Barrie. · I got up early. · I don't remember. (EP — não me lembro, NOT não lembro)
Comparatives & Superlatives
mais ... (do) que = more ... than · menos ... (do) que = less ... than · o/a mais ... = the most ... Special forms: melhor (better), pior (worse), maior (bigger), menor (smaller).
É mais fácil do que pensava. · Ela é a mais inteligente. · Melhor do que ontem.It's easier than I thought. · She's the most intelligent. · Better than yesterday.

Idioms & Fixed Expressions

Everyday Greetings & Social
Tudo bem? / Tudo óptimo?
Everything okay? / Everything great?
Very common greeting. Reply: Tudo bem, obrigado! E tu/você?
Pois pois / Pois é
Yes indeed / I see / Right
Conversation filler. Shows you're listening and agreeing.
Isso!
That's it! / Exactly!
Positive reinforcement. Very natural when something is correct.
Vais bem?
How's it going? / You OK?
Casual, informal check-in. Very natural between friends.
Até à próxima
Until next time / See you next time
Standard farewell. Also: Até logo (see you later), Até já (see you shortly).
Tenha um bom dia
Have a good day
Polite. Tenha = formal/polite command of ter.
De nada
You're welcome
Response to obrigado/obrigada.
Santinho
Bless you (after sneezing)
Literally "little saint". Always use after someone sneezes.
Desculpe / Desculpa
Sorry / Excuse me
Desculpe = formal · Desculpa = informal
Se faz favor
Please
Literally "if you please". Very common in EP, especially when ordering.
Thinking & Expressing Opinion
Acho que...
I think that...
One of the most useful phrases. Acho que está correcto.
Na verdade
Actually / In truth
Na verdade, prefiro café.
Pelo menos
At least
Pelo menos tentei!
Sem dúvida
Without a doubt / Definitely
Strong affirmation.
Tanto faz
Doesn't matter / Either way
Casual. Use when you have no preference.
Pelos vistos / Pelo que parece
Apparently / It seems so
Pelos vistos, vai chover. — Apparently it's going to rain.
Não sei
I don't know
Simple but essential!
Depende
It depends
Universally useful answer.
Emotion & Feeling
Sinto falta de...
I miss... (things / places)
Sinto falta do sol. — I miss the sun.
Tenho saudades de...
I deeply miss...
More emotional, deeper longing than "sinto falta".
Matou saudades

The feeling after finally reuniting with someone you missed.
Tive sorte
I was lucky
Boa sorte! = Good luck! · Que sorte! = How lucky!
É de malucos
It's crazy / It's mad
Colloquial. maluco = madman.

Mild expletive. Raios = thunderbolts.
Que coincidência!
What a coincidence!
Vai mas é embora!
Just go away! / Get out of here!
Playful or serious depending on tone.
Proverbs & Portuguese Wisdom

The Verb Battle
🔄 Ser / Estar / Ficar

One of the most common sources of error. The goal is to get this automatic — no thinking required.

How to play Write 10 sentences about your day using only ser, estar, and ficar. Mix them up. Then try to explain WHY you chose each one. Examples to start: O café ___ frio. · Eu ___ cansado. · Ela ___ médica. · Fiquei ___ surpreendido.

The Café Simulation
🗣️ Speaking practice

Order at a real café entirely in Portuguese. No English allowed, even if the staff switch. This builds real confidence fast — much faster than studying vocabulary lists.

Script to memorise first "Bom dia. Gostaria de [um galão / uma bica / um abatanado], se faz favor." → They respond. → "Quanto custa?" → "Obrigado, até logo."

Por vs Para Sprint
📝 Gap-fill

Fill in the blank — no looking it up first. Then check. The goal is to build intuition, not translate consciously.

Try these (answers at the bottom) 1. Comprei este livro ___ ti.
2. Obrigado ___ tudo.
3. Vou ___ Lisboa amanhã.
4. Trabalhou ___ três horas.
5. Fiz isso ___ aprender.
Answers: 1.para 2.por 3.para 4.por 5.para

Caption the Photo
✍️ Writing practice

Take a photo on your phone — anything around you. Write 5 sentences about it in Portuguese. At least one must use an adjective after the noun. At least one must use ser vs estar correctly. Good for attention to detail.

Self-check before sending ✓ Does every adjective agree with its noun?
✓ Did you use the right ser/estar?
✓ Are the articles correct (o/a/os/as)?

Talk the Streets — Daily Shadowing
🔊 Pronunciation

The Talk the Streets app (free) uses native speakers filmed on actual Portuguese streets. Shadow the audio — play, pause, repeat out loud. The goal is not to understand everything at once, but to train your ear to the rhythm and swallowed vowels of natural EP speech.

Tip 10 minutes of shadowing a day is more effective than 1 hour of grammar study once a week. Consistency beats volume.

Personalised Tips

Based on sessions
Identified in sessions: "Estou a hesitar falar porque falo muito devagar" You know the words — the hesitation is about retrieving them under pressure, not about not knowing them.
🐢
Slow is fine. Stopping isn't.
Speaking confidence

Native speakers never judge a slow learner. They judge silence and apology. Keep going even when the sentence isn't perfect — you will be understood. The goal in natural conversation is communication, not correctness.

Try this rule for one week When you don't know a word: describe it instead of stopping. "A coisa que usamos para beber" (the thing we use to drink) = copo. This is what fluent speakers do when they forget a word too.
Identified in sessions: "Estou muito confiante quando tenho tempo para preparar as palavras" Your confidence is there — it just needs to survive contact with real-time conversation.
🎯
The 5-minute prep before lessons
Lesson preparation

Before each session, spend 5 minutes talking to yourself in Portuguese about what happened that day. You already know how to do this — you're just practising the retrieval speed. By the time the lesson starts, you're already warmed up.

Today's prompt "Hoje acordei às ____. Depois, _____. O tempo está _____. Estou a _____."
Identified in sessions: "Confundi muitos verbos" and "falta de atenção aos detalhes" This is normal at A2–B1 level. The solution is not to study more — it's to slow down the writing process.

The 3-Check Rule
Writing accuracy

After writing anything in Portuguese — a message, a sentence, a WhatsApp — read it back with these three specific questions. Not a general re-read. Specific checks.

The 3 checks 1. Gender: Does each article match the noun? (o/a/os/as)
2. Verb form: Is this the right person and tense?
3. Adjective agreement: Does the adjective match the noun's gender and number?

Passive immersion — RTP Play
Listening

The RTP Play app (free — Portugal's public broadcaster) gives you access to Portuguese news, shows, and documentaries. You don't need to understand everything. Even having it on in the background tunes your ear to the rhythm of European Portuguese.

Start here RTP 1 news at 8pm. Watch for 10 minutes. Don't try to translate — just listen for words you recognise. Write down 3 words you caught. That's a win.
