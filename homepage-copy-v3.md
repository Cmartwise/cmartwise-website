## SECTION: The problem

**Label:** Why it's harder than it should be

**Headline:** The Portuguese you learned and the Portuguese people speak here are basically two different languages.

**Body:**
This is something nobody warns you about before you move. What you get in a classroom, or on Duolingo, or in most apps — it's correct Portuguese. Proper, textbook Portuguese. And then you arrive, and someone at the café says something perfectly normal, and your brain just goes blank, because the sounds are different, words get swallowed, sentences run together in ways the textbook never prepared you for.

I've worked with people who had been living here for years. Taking classes. Using apps. Doing all the things they were told to do. And they still couldn't hold a conversation, because understanding spoken Portuguese and reading written Portuguese are genuinely almost two different skills. Nobody tells you that at the start.

It's not that you're bad at languages. I really believe that. It's that the approach doesn't match what you actually need.

## SECTION: How I work

**Label:** What I actually do

**Headline:** I don't start with a lesson plan. I start with you.

**Body:**
The first thing I ask isn't "what's your level." It's more like — tell me about yourself. How long have you been here? What are the situations that make you nervous? What have you tried before, and what happened? What do you actually want to be able to do?

Because the way I work with you depends entirely on those answers. I don't hand you a programme on the first day. What I build for you, I build specifically for you — and that changes everything.

One of the people I worked with had been in Portugal for six years. She'd even passed the CIPLE exam. And she still couldn't understand what people were saying to her in everyday conversation — because the exam and real life are genuinely quite different. About three months after we started working together, locals were asking her which Portuguese-speaking country she was originally from.

Another person I worked with was putting in real effort — multiple classes a week, hours of practice. But she'd stopped trying to speak at all, because the fear of making a mistake had taken over completely. The work we did together wasn't really about vocabulary or grammar. It was about realising that we all make mistakes in our own language, every single day. Holding yourself to a standard of perfection in a language you've known for a few years isn't dedication. It's just being harder on yourself than you need to be.

Sometimes what gets in the way isn't the language at all.
