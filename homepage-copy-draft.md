## SECTION: The real problem with language learning

**Label:** Why most methods fail

**Headline:** School taught you Portuguese. Portugal didn't.

**Body:**
There's a reason so many expats spend years in Portugal — some taking classes, some using apps, some doing both — and still freeze the moment someone speaks to them at the market or the café.

The way Portuguese is taught bears almost no resemblance to the way Portuguese is actually spoken. The sounds are different. The rhythm is different. The contractions, the dropped syllables, the regional variations — none of that is in the textbook.

Duolingo will teach you vocabulary. A classroom will teach you grammar. Neither will teach you to *understand* a local talking at normal speed, or give you the confidence to open your mouth and try.

That's the gap I work in.

## SECTION: How I work (the philosophy)

**Label:** My approach

**Headline:** I don't have a curriculum. I have you.

**Body:**
The first thing I ask every person I work with isn't "what's your level?" It's "tell me about yourself."

Because how I coach you depends entirely on who you are — your current level, your personality, your daily life in Portugal, what's holding you back, and where you actually want to get to. Then I build everything around that.

Think about how you learned your first language. Nobody handed you a grammar book. You listened. You imitated. You made mistakes constantly and nobody punished you for it. You crawled before you walked. That's the logic I bring back into adult language learning — and it changes everything.

One of my students spent six years in Portugal, passed her CIPLE exam, and still couldn't understand what people were saying to her. The confidence that should have come with that certificate never came, because the test and the real world are two completely different things. Three months later, locals were asking her which Portuguese-speaking country she was originally from.

Another student was spending hours a day studying. Multiple classes a week. And she wasn't speaking at all — not even trying — because the fear of getting it wrong had become bigger than the desire to communicate. What shifted wasn't her grammar. It was the realisation that we make mistakes in our own language every single day. Holding yourself to a standard of perfection in a language you've known for three years isn't discipline. It's just unnecessary pain.

Sometimes, learning a language has nothing to do with knowledge.
