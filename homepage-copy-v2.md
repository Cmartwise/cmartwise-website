## SECTION: The problem

**Label:** Why it's not your fault

**Headline:** You weren't taught the wrong language. You were taught the wrong version of it.

**Body:**
Here's something nobody tells you before you move to Portugal: the Portuguese you learn in a classroom, or on Duolingo, or in any app, is not the Portuguese people actually speak here.

The sounds are different. Words get swallowed. Sentences run together in ways that make no sense if you learned from a book. And then you arrive, someone says something perfectly normal to you, and your brain just... stops.

That's not a you problem. That's a method problem.

I've seen people spend years here — taking classes, using apps, doing everything they were told to do — and still freeze at the checkout or the doctor's office because understanding spoken Portuguese and reading written Portuguese are almost two different skills.

That's what I work on. The real thing.

## SECTION: How I work

**Label:** My approach

**Headline:** I don't start with a lesson plan. I start with you.

**Body:**
The first question I ask isn't "what's your level?" It's "tell me about yourself."

Where are you in your life here? What do you actually need to be able to say? What's making you avoid speaking, or avoid situations where you'd have to? What have you tried, and why didn't it stick?

Because the way I coach you depends entirely on the answers to those questions. There's no standard programme I hand you on day one. What I build for you, I build for you specifically.

I think about it like this: when you learned your first language as a child, nobody handed you a grammar book. You listened constantly, you copied sounds you didn't understand yet, you said things wrong a hundred times before you said them right, and nobody made you feel bad about it. That process worked because it was natural — and it's the same logic I bring back in.

One of the people I've worked with had lived in Portugal for six years. She'd passed her CIPLE exam. And she still couldn't understand what people were saying to her — because the exam and the real world are two completely different things. Three months later, locals were asking her which Portuguese-speaking country she was originally from.

Another person was putting in hours every day. Multiple classes a week. And she'd stopped trying to speak at all — because the fear of getting something wrong had completely taken over. What changed wasn't her vocabulary or her grammar. It was realising that we make mistakes in our own language every single day. Holding yourself to a standard of perfection in a language you've known for a few years isn't dedication. It's just being hard on yourself for no good reason.

Sometimes the work isn't about language at all.
